Journalism

My work has focused primarily on legislative analysis and public accountability.

I dig deep into topics in order to write news that is fair.

Complaints and Citations Rise Sharply at Veritas Apartments Cited in Lawsuit

Tenants alleged that Veritas Investments Inc., perhaps the largest rent-control landlord in San Francisco, neglected their needs while fostering inhospitable living environments through perennial, on-site construction projects — with the goal of pushing tenants out and charging newcomers higher rents. The company vigorously denied the claims.


In my story, I created a custom database from various public records, proving that building-code violations increased after the company bought the properties. This appeared to lend credence to the tenants’ narrative.


How We Tracked Down the Information to Answer Questions About Tenant Complaints

My analysis of Veritas properties drew public data from multiple sources, synthesizing them into a single data set that allowed me to spot trends.


I ran my analytical approach by both the real estate company and one of the city’s chief building inspectors [DBL CHECK], neither of whom criticized it.


Remaking Rent Control — if Voters Approve

In late 2018, California voters prepared to weigh in on a controversial ballot measure that could strengthen local rent-control policies.


I dissected a form of rent control that would become legal if the state measure passed, and that had potential to appease warring political camps dramatically growing the stock of price-controlled housing.


More Homeless Returning to Streets From Navigation Centers

City officials were touting a new type of homelenessness shelter, called a “Navigation Center,” as a major tool for helping people get off the streets.


But my data analysis proved that the centers’ clients were increasingly returning to the streets, and the largest subset of clients received bus rides out of town.


Earthquake Retrofit Delays Leave Thousands at Risk

Years prior, city officials had commissioned a study to estimate how many, and which, apartment buildings were vulnerable to collapse in an earthquake. But they never publicized the findings.


When I did, the resulting web traffic overloaded our site for days. Within months, the city had created a program to compel those building owners to retrofit.


Workflow and Processes

Word was falling through the cracks ― I figured out how to fill them. This required locating the appropriate tools, customizing them to fit our needs and getting buy-in from the team to adopt them.

Problem: “Where did you put that file?”

Folders and files were stored all over the place: In various team members’ personal hard drives or cloud-storage accounts. Files were unnecessarily lost or duplicated ― often.


Solution: Box.com

I pulled together files and folders into a single, coherent file structure. I found a cloud-storage product that suited our budget and allowed for granular security controls, and trained the team how to use it.

Problem: “You were supposed to file that draft a week ago!”

We frequently got deep into our multi-month reporting projects without accomplishing milestone goals, such as hitting story deadlines or shooting enough photos.


Solution: Airtable

I customized pages (technically, “bases”) in Airtable that were sorted, filtered, and possessed specialized fields and views for individual team members. This allowed each person to engage with a subset of the tool, but their inputs combined into We then traded off the responsibility of managing this database of to-dos.

Problem: “I don’t recall you telling me that...”

Communication was strewn across email and SMS tools, and these conversations often left out necessary people. This led to confusion, frustration, delays.


Solution: Slack

I carefully tailored communication channels, focusing each on a topic that needed its own breathing room. I trained each team member how to use the tool to minimize crosstalk.
